Hebrew Bible


The Hebrew Bible, known to Christians as the Old Testament, is a collection of ancient Hebrew writings, most of them composed in the 8th through 2nd centuries BCE, mostly in ancient Hebrew, with some of the later parts composed in Aramaic.

Topics, points of view, even genres vary, from the mythological and legendary tales such as those found in Genesis and Exodus, to more historical narratives of Samuel and Kings, to prophetical verse or prose such as in Isaiah or Ezekiel, to the praiseful poems of Psalms, to short stories such as Esther or Ruth, and plenty others.

The Old Testament

Jesus of Nazareth, as well as the first of what we might call Christians, were Jewish, and as such, adhered to Jewish scriptures. Although within a century the new religion split off from Judaism and an increasing majority of Christians were non-Jewish, still, for various reasons, these old scriptures continued to be considered scripture.

Place in canon

The Hebrew Bible is relevant today to Jews and Christians in differing ways. There are also some differences between the Jewish and Christian canons in regard to the Hebrew Bible, which is the group of texts that these two religions share.
